Where The Water Goes

A Murray-Darling Primer

Why Care About The Murray-Darling Basin?

The waterways flow from highlands, like Kosciuszko National Park and the Great Dividing Range in Southern Queensland, but all of Basin’s tributaries end up in the one location: at the mouth of the Murray river in South Australia.

It's Long

The world’s 15th longest river system, it culmiantes to be a thousand kilometres longer than the Zambesi in Africa.

And It's Big

The Murray-Darling Basin covers an area nearly two times the size of France. 2

But it is fragile

It is an extremely vulnerable river system, as most of the rainfall it gets each year is lost to evaporation. The Murray Darling Basin covers the same area as the Indus Basin which spans parts of India and Pakistan. While the Indus Basin loses 10% of rainfall to evaporation, the Murray-Darling loses a whopping 94%. 3

The rainfall varies considerably each year, with cyclical droughts and floods

Crops Cotton socks and laser beams

Over the years farmers have carved out canals and levees to extract water from the rivers and the majority of water is used for Basin crops. Planted crops alone were worth more than $9.5 billion in 2005-06.

Gross value of agricultural production in the Murray-Darling Basin — 2005–06

Technology heading

To best utilise the scarce water of the region, farmers have needed to develop sophisticated technologies. Some use satellite farming techniques, whereby they gather data via satellite mapping; knowing the soil moisture in a paddock a half-hour's drive away can be done with a click of a button.

Although long maligned as too thirsty to grow in Australia, the rice crops of the Basin have the highest yields in the world. To save water, farmers use a laser to accurately level the paddocks. The end result is a flat, slightly slanted paddock where a smaller amount of water can flow across, get to the end, and be pumped back to the top to use again. 3

Controversial cotton

Last decade the cotton crop consumed around 20% of all irrigated water from the basin. Yet a sample year (2005-06) shows that cotton only contributed 6% of the basin's production in dollars. 3

Livestock & Dairy Wooly jumpers and your morning coffee

Australia’s economy once bounced around “on the sheep’s back”, with the world wool trade well and truly stitched up by the Aussies in the 1800s. The nation still commands over a quarter of the world’s wool trade. 3

Where does your latte come from?

If you’re drinking your milky morning coffee in NSW or Victoria, it’s highly likely to be from a Basin cow, as one-third of the dairy cows in Australia are hoofing around the fertile plains of the Basin. 3

Thirsty stock

More water is used for farming livestock than you would think, with farmers needing to draw extra water from underground sources and irrigation, especially in times of drought. Also, if rainfall is not supporting decent pasture growth for the livestock’s feed, they must be fed hay, which might require irrigated water to grow. The Basin supplies 44% of Australia’s hay for livestock. 3

The rest... Is the glass half full?

The remaining Basin water is consumed as drinking water, lost during transportation, or used for mining and industrial activity. While mining uses only a small proportion of the Basin’s water capital there are ongoing concerns about the mines’ affect on the health of the river system. 3

Drink up

Over three million people rely on the Murray Darling Basin for drinking water. The two largest Basin drinking cities of Canberra and Adelaide are at different extremes of the river system: Canberra sources its water at the upper end of the system, from subsidiaries of the Murrumbidgee river; and Adelaide gets its water from the end of the Murray River, after all of the other subsidiary rivers have flowed into it, and irrigators have taken water out of it.

A good gauge of the taste of the water is to measure the concentration of calcium carbonate in the drinking water. Higher levels of calcium carbonate mean that the water is ‘hard’ and has a strong mineral taste, whereas ‘soft’ water tastes flatter, or most would say better. 3

Lost in Translation

Another controversial issue for the Basin is the large amount of water that is ‘lost’ during transmission. This is water that doesn’t get used for agriculture, as somewhere during its transportation from the river to the paddock it has either evaporated, seeped through a canal bank, flooded over the river bank onto a floodplain, or a bevy of issues caused by weaknesses in the irrigation infrastructure. Environmentalists argue that this water is not ‘lost’, and is actually naturally returning to the environment – topping up acquifers and maintaining natural floodplains and other important ecological considerations. 3
